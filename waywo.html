Designing Waywo from 0 → 1 and getting our first enterprise customer.

Waywo on Desktop

The Problem

Imagine you are a CEO at a 1000-person company, how would you know what people are working on?

Meetings? Slack DMs? Asking your team?

Sure, that might work. But as teams grow, it becomes a massive challenge to understand what people are working on, and if they have challenges or blockers that need your attention.

This can be a problem across the entire company, but it was much bigger for executives because they have to make decisions that affect the entire company.

Waywo - an acronym for "what are you working on" solves this problem.

Leaders have to deal with a lot of chaos in their day-to-day. There are so many tools and conversations happening that it's hard to keep track of everything.

My Role & Team

As a Senior Product Designer, I led the design of Waywo from 0 → 1, collaborating with the CEO, co-founder, and a cross-functional team to deliver the MVP.

Timeline: Aug - Sep 2024 (4 weeks)

My Responsibilities:

  • Product Strategy
  • Workshop Faciliation
  • UX and UI Design
  • User Research

    • My Process

    After listening to 15+ customer interviews, I created a product strategy, facilitated ideation sessions, and prototyped, a lot.

    To understand the problem better, I spent a few days listening to customer interviews done by the CEO and co-founder. From there, I created a product strategy to prioritize features we knew would resonate with customers and that people would be willing to pay for.

    Research Synthesis

    Decisions are often made in tools like Slack, GitHub, or Jira. Executives often find out when it's too late.

    Most conversations are done in Slack/Microsoft Teams or meetings, but most of it filtered and not seen by execs. And because they’re so busy, when things go off-track, they find out too late - often weeks later. This leads to decisions being made that end up being extremely expensive for the business.

    Founders and executives wish if they could just have known earlier, they may have been able to support their team better.

    Interview recordings from founders and executives.

    Creating our Product Strategy

    Good strategy is focus, and I wanted to make sure my team is aligned from the start.

    I created a strategy informed by the reseach insights. Collaborating with my team, it helped us prioritize, align, and build something that people wanted and would pay for.

    From a design lens, I wanted to make sure even the MVP was a delight. There is so much boring software, I wanted to make sure Waywo would have elements that bring joy.

    Miro board to map the value/effort canvas and help my team discuss strategy.

    Initial concepts

    I created a few different concepts, constantly sharing for my team's feedback.

    Some of the early concepts varied from Slack app and email summary notifications. With the goal to build a daily a habit product, inspiration came from social media to see stories of team members and a real-time feed of their company.

    After a week of concept explorations, I was really happy with one option that I thought was going to be a winner.

    But I was about to find out...

    Some early concepts included facebook-like feed, stories, email notifications, and a slack app.

    Early feedback and disagreements

    We met in-person for a working session, and the feedback was not what I expected...

    We discussed strategy, brainstormed ideas, and I started presenting some of the concepts I had come up with.

    I presented three concepts that I was really excited about. The first was a Slack app that would send summaries to executives. The second was an email summary that would be sent daily or weekly. The third was a social app that would have a feed like facebook and stories like instagram.

    There was a moment of silence from the team.. Then Tyler (CEO) said:

    Tyler: "I don’t like any of them."

    I wasn't sure why, so I asked for an explanation.

    Me: "Why not?"

    Tyler: "It’s not structuring the chaos that leaders have to deal with, it's creating more..."

    Later that day, while driving my colleague Juan (an AI Engineer) home, we got into a discussion about large language models like Claude 3.5 Sonnet — their limitations, strengths, and how we might leverage them. That conversation sparked a new idea.

    The sentiment towards the early concepts.

    The aha moment

    Iterating with a new perspective.

    Driving back home from the team on-site, I had lightbulbs going on in my head with ideas.

    And an idea came to mind.

    What if we visualized the summaries as if someone was giving you a written memo in person? I sketched out the idea and shared it with the team.

    The whole team liked this approach. It solved the problem much more simply and elegant way. We validated this by testing it with other executives who also thought this solved their problem effectively.

    We were ready to build our MVP.

    Some iterations from sketching to final MVP designs.

    Collaboration

    I worked closely with the engineers to build the MVP.

    During planning and strategy, we knew our MVP would be simple and slightly hacky. The team wanted to send summary CSV files to customers but I suggested that’s not very user friendly. So we built a UI for desktop and mobile.

    I used Loom as an async tool to collaborate with my team.

    Outcomes

    Our first enterprise customer!

    Tyler (CEO) sent a video of the moment our first customer was signing the deal. These early customers became our design partners, offering regular feedback to help evolve the product.

    The product evolved at a fast pace — weekly feedback loops allowed the team to iterate often. This project provided real potential to drive revenue, attract funding, and ultimately, save the company.

    The whole team was excited!

    Reflections

    A lot of learnings...

    These were some of the most exhausting yet rewarding weeks in my career. Dealing with the ambiguity of a 0 → 1 product, and leveraging AI in a product taught me a lot about how to navigate this process.

    One big takeaway was approaching challenges by solving "million-dollar problems" as Tyler (CEO) always advocated to the team. Focusing on significant problems that provide tangible value is what drives investment and business success.

    It was a delight to work with my team. In hindsight, I should have collaborated with AI Engineer even earlier to understand our AI constraints because that's what allowed the product to come to life.

    Some memories from the project: AI output review, workshops, loom video for feedback, early concepts...
