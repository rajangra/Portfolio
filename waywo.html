Designing Waywo from 0 → 1 and getting our first enterprise customer.

Waywo on Desktop

The Problem

Imagine you are a CEO at a 1000-person company, how would you know what people are working on?

Meetings? Slack DMs? Asking your team?

Sure, that might work. But as teams grow, it becomes a massive challenge to understand what people are working on, and if they have challenges or blockers that need your attention.

This can be a problem across the entire company, but it was much bigger for executives because they have to make decisions that affect the entire company.

Waywo - an acronym for "what are you working on" solves this problem.

Leaders have to deal with a lot of chaos in their day-to-day. There are so many tools and conversations happening that it's hard to keep track of everything.

My Role & Team

As a Senior Product Designer, I led the design of Waywo from 0 → 1, collaborating with the CEO, co-founder, and a cross-functional team to deliver the MVP.

Timeline: Aug - Sep 2024 (4 weeks)

My Responsibilities:

  • Product Strategy
  • Workshop Facilitation
  • UX and UI Design
  • User Research

    • My Process

    After listening to 15+ customer interviews, I created a product strategy, facilitated ideation sessions, and prototyped, a lot.

    To understand the problem better, I spent a few days listening to customer interviews done by the CEO and co-founder. From there, I created a product strategy to prioritize features we knew would resonate with customers and that people would be willing to pay for.

    Working closely with the AI engineer helped ensure that I fully understood the constraints and pushed the possibilities of this new technology. Finally, I worked closely with front-end and back-end engineers to deliver the MVP on both web and responsive mobile.

    Research Synthesis

    Decisions are often made in tools like Slack, GitHub, or Jira, but executives find out when it's too late.

    Conversations happening within teams are often filtered out. Executives are often unaware of issues until they’ve escalated, which can lead to costly decisions for the business.

    Founders and executives wish they could’ve been informed earlier to better support their teams.

    Interview recordings from founders and executives.

    Creating our Product Strategy

    A good strategy is all about focus, so I worked to align my team from the start.

    I created a strategy based on research insights, which helped us prioritize and align on features that customers would value and pay for.

    From a design perspective, I wanted the MVP to be delightful. Something that stood out from the typical boring software. I aimed to incorporate elements that brought joy into the user experience.

    A Miro board helped us map out the value/effort canvas and discuss strategy.

    Initial concepts

    I developed several concepts and continuously shared them for team feedback.

    Early concepts included a Slack app, email notifications, and a social-media-like feed, inspired by the idea of creating a daily habit product. I also considered real-time updates similar to social media stories.

    After a week of exploration, I was excited about one concept that I thought would be the winner.

    But then, I received some feedback on it...

    Some early concepts included Facebook-like feed, stories, email notifications, and a slack app.

    Early feedback and disagreements

    During an in-person session, I presented my concepts, but the feedback wasn’t what I expected.

    We brainstormed and discussed ideas, and I presented three concepts I was excited about: a Slack app for sending summaries to executives, a daily or weekly email summary, and a social app with a Facebook-like feed and Instagram-like stories.

    There was a moment of silence from the team.. Then Tyler (CEO) said:

    Tyler: "I don’t like any of them."

    I wasn't sure why, so I asked for an explanation.

    Me: "Why not?"

    Tyler: "It’s not structuring the chaos that leaders deal with—it’s adding to it..."

    Later that day, while driving my colleague Juan (AI Engineer) home, we discussed large language models like Claude 3.5 and how we might leverage them. That conversation sparked a new idea.

    The sentiment towards early concepts.

    The aha moment

    Iterating with a new perspective.

    On my way back home from the team on-site, I had lightbulbs going off in my head with ideas.

    And an idea came to mind.

    What if we visualized the summaries as if someone were giving you a memo in person? I quickly sketched the idea and shared it with the team.

    The team loved this approach—it solved the problem more simply and elegantly. We validated it with other executives, and they also found it effective.

    We were ready to build our MVP.

    Some iterations from sketching to final MVP designs.

    Collaboration

    I collaborated closely with engineers to build the MVP.

    While the team initially suggested sending summary CSV files to customers, I recommended building a more user-friendly interface. So, we developed a UI for both desktop and mobile.

    I used Loom as an async tool to collaborate with my team.

    Outcomes

    We landed our first enterprise customer!

    Tyler (CEO) shared a video of the moment the deal was signed. These early customers became design partners, providing valuable feedback that helped us evolve the product.

    With weekly feedback loops, we iterated quickly, and the project began to show real potential to drive revenue, and attract further funding.

    The whole team was excited!

    Reflections

    The process wasn’t easy, but it was incredibly rewarding.

    The ambiguity of building a 0 → 1 product and leveraging AI taught me how to navigate complex challenges.

    A key takeaway was Tyler's focus on solving "million-dollar problems" — focusing on issues that provide significant value is what drives investment and business success.

    It was a pleasure working with the team. In hindsight, I wish I’d collaborated with the AI engineer earlier to understand the technology’s constraints, as that’s what brought the product to life.

    Some memories from the project: AI output review, workshops, loom video for feedback, early concepts...
