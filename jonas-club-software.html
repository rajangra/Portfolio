Context

Jonas Club Software provides software for private clubs of all types, including golf, country, city, and yacht clubs. They have been a global leader for over 30 years, serving 2300+ clubs in 20 countries.

But over the last decade, the numbers began to tell a different story.

Since 2015, Jonas Club Software has lost 50 to 100 customers every year.
Millions of dollars in annual recurring revenue, gone.

Why? The new generation of users didn't want to use software that felt like it was stuck in the 1990s. This led to a high employee turnover, which made clubs leave Jonas Club Software for the competition.

Our goal was clear: Build a modern, best-in-class Dining Reservations & Table Management System, and reclaim the #1 choice for club software.

My Role & Team

As the first designer at a 30+ year old company, I had the opportunity to rethink how product and UX operated internally.

I introduced the design and research process, and contributed across roles including product management, research, design, and frontend development. I aligned stakeholders around a clear product direction and built the design system and coded components that now serve as the foundation for future products across the company.

Team:

Timeline: Jun 2025 - Jan 2026

1. Planning

The project was ambiguous, so I started with a project plan.

I find this helpful to communicate how we’ll move from start to finish, keeping stakeholders informed and aligned throughout.

2. Discovery & Research

Helping the team make better decisions.

Since there was no previous research or design practice at Jonas Club Software, I knew introducing this was critical to the success of the project. For years, project managers relied on personal and anecdotal opinions to build software. But I wanted to be user-centric from the start.

I started with secondary research to better understand users' needs, their goals, and competitors like OpenTable and Toast.

This formed the foundation for the rest of the project.

2b. Primary Research

Getting close to the users with interviews & contextual inquiries.

Secondary research was the starting point, but getting evidence from real users was going to guide prioritization and future design decisions.

We had a number of key insights which shaped the rest of the process:

  • While the existing system had a ton of features, it was incredibly difficult to set up and use.
  • The product had critical features missing like Table Management, which was essential to running restaurant F&B operations.
  • Lack of support for iPad and mobile meant the staff was stuck using a fixed-location computer which was extremely inefficient and inflexible.

3. Prioritization

Users wanted all the features, but ruthless prioritization is what would bring revenue.

The users wanted a lot of new functionality that Jonas Club Software did not currently support. There was no way we could implement everything with the limited resources, so I helped the team prioritize high-impact areas that would help us win new clients once we launch the product.

Here I initially leveraged AI to create requirements, which were further refined from real research insights. This helped us plan for long-term vision, roadmaps, and breaking down into smaller projects and milestones (MVP, M2, etc)

4. Design

Designing the core Dining Reservations & Table Management experience for Clubs.

Note: This was before AI prototyping became common, which has since changed my workflow significantly.

With the MVP requirements clear, I explored different ways to design the experience in low-fidelity while building the design system at the same time.

As the flows were refined and the design system took shape, I turned wireframes into mockups and prototypes for usability testing.

I shared progress in daily standups and weekly meetings to get feedback from the team.

4b. Prototype

Preparing a high-fidelity prototype for usability testing.

As a team, we knew the host experience was the biggest risk. If we got this wrong, nothing else would matter. Getting it right was key to setting our product apart and winning.

From floor plans and table management to a smooth booking flow, here is a preview of the final Jonas Dining Reservations system.

5. Usability Testing

Evaluating our assumptions with real users.

With the prototype ready, I created a usability test plan to guide the sessions. We tested it with over 12 clubs and several internal stakeholders.

Feedback from club staff helped us refine the details before building in code. Because of the early research, no major changes were needed.

With that, we were ready to build!

6. Build

Collaborating with the backend developer.

While we looked for a frontend developer in the team, I was keen to take on the task of building the design system in code.

As the project progressed, we still didn't have a frontend developer, so I put my developer hat on, and leveraged AI to build the frontend of the app.

One challenge was that the staff wanted the system to work as a native iPad app, and also work on desktop and mobile.

I had the idea to build the app as a Progressive Web App (PWA) which allowed the system to run like a native app on iPad, but also responsive on other devices. This saved us a lot of time and effort, and positioned our product as one that works across all platforms.

7. Outcomes

The big reveal at CMAA 2026.

My favorite part of the process came at the annual CMAA show, the world’s biggest club event. One of our team members demoed the system to over 120 clubs that signed up for it.

To summarize, they all said the same thing: “When can we have it?”

With an average ARR of $25,000 per club, that meant a potential $3.1 million in new revenue in the first year alone.
